Ally Insights: Accessibility & Inclusive Design

As part of my portfolio, this site demonstrates my commitment to building an inclusive web. This project explores semantic HTML, accessibility structure, and plain language design.

Good design is not just visual—it is structural, readable, and accessible for all users, including those using assistive technologies.

Structuring Accessible Content

Accessible documents depend on strong semantic structure. HTML elements create meaning, not just layout.

  • Use proper language attributes for screen readers
  • Follow logical heading order (H1 → H2 → H3)
  • Use landmarks like main, nav, section, and footer correctly

Navigation & Page Flow

Navigation landmarks allow users to move through content efficiently without linear reading.

  • Main navigation defines site structure
  • Breadcrumbs show hierarchy
  • Local navigation improves page scanning

DOM order determines keyboard navigation—not visual layout.

U.S. Disabilities Overview

Approximately 13% of U.S. adults report a disability, with significant disparities in employment outcomes.

Forms & Interaction Design

Forms are critical interaction points and should always be easy to locate and understand.

  • Label all inputs clearly
  • Group related fields logically
  • Mark search and login areas explicitly

Labor Force Participation Insights

This chart shows labor force participation differences between disabled and non-disabled populations.

  • Lowest participation occurs in ages 16–19
  • Largest gaps appear during working-age years
  • Participation declines near retirement

Plain Language Communication

Plain language improves clarity and ensures users can quickly understand content.

Identify Your Audience

  • Who are your readers?
  • What do they need?
  • What do they already know?

Write Clearly

  • Use short sentences
  • Prefer everyday words
  • Avoid unnecessary complexity

Evaluate Content

  • Proofread carefully
  • Ensure readability
  • Remove unnecessary wording
